# The Six Pillars of Perfect Money

The backbone of most educational DGD videos. Synthesized from Menger, Mises, and Hayek (WP §2.2). Each pillar is a ready-made short-form topic.

> Teach these as **economic concepts**. They make excellent, evergreen, non-promotional content. You can explain a pillar fully without ever telling anyone to acquire DGD.

| # | Pillar | The idea | DGD's design answer (WP §2.2) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Scarcity** | Supply must be limited by transparent, predictable rules | 21M cap (19M circulating, 2M treasury); cannot be increased |
| 2 | **Stable Pricing** | Purchasing power should stay roughly constant | Price advances along a fixed curve tied to measurable adoption, not speculation |
| 3 | **Free Adoption** | Money must be chosen, not imposed by decree | Participation is voluntary; users choose when/whether/how much |
| 4 | **Decentralized Governance** | Rules, not rulers; no single authority can alter policy | On-chain consensus; rules fixed at inception, unchangeable |
| 5 | **Freedom to Transact** | Use without surveillance, censorship, or permission | Native Tor V3 onion support for private transactions |
| 6 | **Adequate Circulation** | Must actually flow through the economy at sufficient velocity | Single-price design + supply-chain onboarding to keep it circulating |

## Pillar-by-pillar (with teachable hooks)

### 1. Scarcity
The Fed expanded the US money supply from ~$3 billion in 1913 to over $21 trillion — roughly a **7,000% increase** (WP §2.2). Bitcoin proved code-enforced scarcity is possible with its 21M cap. DGD adopts the same 21M cap.
**Hook idea:** "The dollar in your pocket is designed to shrink. Here's the math."

### 2. Stable Pricing
A 2% inflation target means the dollar loses **half its purchasing power about every 35 years** (WP §2.2). Bitcoin appreciates long-term but swings violently short-term, which hurts its use as everyday money. DGD's design aims for predictability by tying price to adoption along a fixed curve.
**Hook idea:** "Why your grandparents' $100 bought a month of groceries and yours buys a week."

### 3. Free Adoption
No cryptocurrency was forced on anyone — unlike national currencies backed by legal tender laws. DGD extends this: its distribution mechanism is equally voluntary.
**Hook idea:** "Nobody can force you to use a currency. So why do we all use the same one?"

### 4. Decentralized Governance
"Rules rather than discretion." DGD's rules were set at inception and **cannot be changed** by any single authority (WP §2.2, §12.14). The White Paper calls this the *"inversion of governance"* — there's nothing to govern because nothing changes (WP §12.15).
**Hook idea:** "What if money had rules no government could rewrite overnight?"

### 5. Freedom to Transact
Money should let you transact "without surveillance, censorship, or permission from any intermediary." DGD integrates Tor V3 onion addressing for encrypted, anonymous transactions (WP §2.2).
**Hook idea:** "Every card swipe is watched. Cash isn't. Where does digital money fit?"

### 6. Adequate Circulation
This is DGD's signature argument. Real money doesn't just go consumer→merchant; it flows merchant→wholesaler→manufacturer→raw-material supplier→workers. A coin instantly converted to dollars never became money — it was just a payment instrument (WP Abstract, §9). DGD's single-price design is built to let it circulate the full chain.
**Hook idea:** "Most 'crypto payments' aren't really money. Here's the test that proves it."
