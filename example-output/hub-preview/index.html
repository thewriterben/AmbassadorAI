What Is Digital Gold (DGD)?

The subject you teach. A plain-language overview of Digital Gold for ambassadors, drawn from the White Paper.

Framing reminder: Everything below is how the system is designed. Present it as design and ideas, never as "buy this, you'll profit." See communications discipline.

In one breath

Digital Gold (DGD) is a Layer-1 cryptocurrency designed to behave like sound, wealth-preserving money rather than a speculative bet. It was created by John Wright Gotts, founder of the Digital Gold Foundation, and developed from 2014–2026 (WP, title page). The founder funded development personally and donated the completed coin to the Foundation (WP Abstract).

A "Layer-1" is a base blockchain network (like Bitcoin or Ethereum) that runs on its own, rather than being built on top of another chain.

The problem it responds to

Government-issued money loses value over time. The White Paper notes the Federal Reserve Note has lost more than 96% of its purchasing power since 1913, and that a 2%-per-year inflation target means a worker saving across a 40-year career sees roughly half of their lifetime savings eroded by debasement (WP Abstract). DGD is framed as a response to that problem, inspired by economist Friedrich Hayek's idea (The Denationalisation of Money, 1976) that private currencies should compete, and the best one for users wins.

What makes DGD structurally different

  1. Built to be spent, not just held. Most coins have a fluctuating bid/ask price, so a merchant who accepts them converts to dollars immediately. DGD uses a single-price architecture so a merchant can hold it and pay suppliers in it — letting it circulate through a whole supply chain (WP Abstract, §9). See supply & distribution.
  2. Fair, continuous distribution. Instead of concentrating coins among early insiders, a Proof-of-Participation (PoP) model releases coins gradually as the network grows from 1,000 toward 80 million users (WP Abstract). See supply & distribution.
  3. A real valuation method. The Crypto Fair Value (CFV) formula values coins against a fixed benchmark instead of pure speculation. See valuation: CFV & DGSB.
  4. Designed not to be a security. Fixed rules set at inception that can't be changed, no staking rewards, fees burned, no ongoing company profiting from operation (WP Abstract, §12). See compliance overview.

The intellectual roots (great explainer material)

DGD draws on the Austrian school of economics — this is some of the most "teachable," non-promotional content you can make:

  • Carl Menger (1892): money emerges naturally from trade, not government decree; the most saleable goods (durable, divisible, portable, scarce, widely wanted) become money — historically gold and silver.
  • Ludwig von Mises: government monopoly over money leads to debasement and a wealth transfer from workers to the financial class.
  • Friedrich Hayek (1976): strip the monopoly; let currencies compete.

These ideas synthesize into the Six Pillars of Perfect Money — the backbone of most educational DGD videos.

The four ways people participate in DGD, and the one that defines what you do as a content-creating ambassador.

You are the "Promotional participation" pathway

The White Paper defines it directly (WP §10.1):

"Participants who post DGD-related content publicly, including educational explanations, technical analyses, commentary on the framework, or other contributions to the public conversation about DGD, may earn DGD recognition through programs the Foundation operates. … The promotional pathway rewards the work of public discourse that grows the broader awareness of DGD."

Note the word the White Paper uses for what you make: educational explanations, technical analyses, commentary. Not sales pitches. The pathway exists to grow awareness and understanding, not to drive purchases.

Specific recognition structures are announced through the Foundation's official channels — primarily @DigitalGoldTalk on X (WP §10.1). This wiki does not set or promise any recognition amounts; check official channels.

The compliance consequence you must understand

Because the promotional pathway can pay you DGD recognition for posting, you have a material connection to the Foundation in the eyes of the FTC and the platforms. That means:

  1. Your content is treated as branded/commercial content, and you must disclose it. → FTC disclosure
  2. You're still bound by the Foundation's communications discipline — educational, never investment framing.
  3. Platform financial-promotion rules apply to you. → platform policies

This is not a reason to avoid the work — it's a reason to do it cleanly. Disclosed, educational content is exactly what the pathway is designed to reward.

  • Walking through the CFV idea or the single-price circulation argument.
  • Commentary on monetary history, inflation, Austrian economics.
  • Honest "here's how the design works" technical breakdowns.

The Six Pillars of Perfect Money

The backbone of most educational DGD videos. Synthesized from Menger, Mises, and Hayek (WP §2.2). Each pillar is a ready-made short-form topic.

Teach these as economic concepts. They make excellent, evergreen, non-promotional content. You can explain a pillar fully without ever telling anyone to acquire DGD.

# Pillar The idea DGD's design answer (WP §2.2)
1 Scarcity Supply must be limited by transparent, predictable rules 21M cap (19M circulating, 2M treasury); cannot be increased
2 Stable Pricing Purchasing power should stay roughly constant Price advances along a fixed curve tied to measurable adoption, not speculation
3 Free Adoption Money must be chosen, not imposed by decree Participation is voluntary; users choose when/whether/how much
4 Decentralized Governance Rules, not rulers; no single authority can alter policy On-chain consensus; rules fixed at inception, unchangeable
5 Freedom to Transact Use without surveillance, censorship, or permission Native Tor V3 onion support for private transactions
6 Adequate Circulation Must actually flow through the economy at sufficient velocity Single-price design + supply-chain onboarding to keep it circulating

Pillar-by-pillar (with teachable hooks)

1. Scarcity

The Fed expanded the US money supply from ~$3 billion in 1913 to over $21 trillion — roughly a 7,000% increase (WP §2.2). Bitcoin proved code-enforced scarcity is possible with its 21M cap. DGD adopts the same 21M cap. Hook idea: "The dollar in your pocket is designed to shrink. Here's the math."

2. Stable Pricing

A 2% inflation target means the dollar loses half its purchasing power about every 35 years (WP §2.2). Bitcoin appreciates long-term but swings violently short-term, which hurts its use as everyday money. DGD's design aims for predictability by tying price to adoption along a fixed curve. Hook idea: "Why your grandparents' $100 bought a month of groceries and yours buys a week."

3. Free Adoption

No cryptocurrency was forced on anyone — unlike national currencies backed by legal tender laws. DGD extends this: its distribution mechanism is equally voluntary. Hook idea: "Nobody can force you to use a currency. So why do we all use the same one?"

4. Decentralized Governance

"Rules rather than discretion." DGD's rules were set at inception and cannot be changed by any single authority (WP §2.2, §12.14). The White Paper calls this the "inversion of governance" — there's nothing to govern because nothing changes (WP §12.15). Hook idea: "What if money had rules no government could rewrite overnight?"

5. Freedom to Transact

Money should let you transact "without surveillance, censorship, or permission from any intermediary." DGD integrates Tor V3 onion addressing for encrypted, anonymous transactions (WP §2.2). Hook idea: "Every card swipe is watched. Cash isn't. Where does digital money fit?"

6. Adequate Circulation

This is DGD's signature argument. Real money doesn't just go consumer→merchant; it flows merchant→wholesaler→manufacturer→raw-material supplier→workers. A coin instantly converted to dollars never became money — it was just a payment instrument (WP Abstract, §9). DGD's single-price design is built to let it circulate the full chain. Hook idea: "Most 'crypto payments' aren't really money. Here's the test that proves it."

How to turn this into a series

Six pillars = a 6-part video series, one pillar per video, plus an intro ("the 6 things real money needs") and an outro ("how one project tries to hit all six"). See series ideas.

DGD as a "Safe Harbor" Coin

One of DGD's strongest — and most misunderstood — talking points. Get the meaning right and it's a great educational topic. Get it wrong and you walk straight into the one trap this whole wiki warns about.

Read this box twice. "Safe harbor" here is a legal/regulatory term — it means DGD is designed to fall outside securities law. It does NOT mean DGD is a "safe investment," a "safe bet," or a "safe haven" for your money. Those are investment claims and they're banned (communications discipline). Worse: framing DGD as a profit play destroys the very classification that puts it in the safe harbor. More on that below.

What "safe harbor" actually means (the legal meaning)

A regulatory safe harbor is a set of conditions that, if you meet them, keep you on the right side of a law. For crypto, the question is always: is this token a security? (If yes, it triggers heavy securities regulation.)

The White Paper (§12.8) ties DGD to two 2026 developments:

  1. The March 17, 2026 Joint Interpretive Release — a five-part taxonomy of crypto assets: digital commodities, digital collectibles, digital tools, payment stablecoins (under the GENIUS Act), and digital securities. The first four are explicitly NOT securities; only "digital securities" (tokenized stocks/bonds) are (WP §12.7).
  2. The Atkins "Regulation Crypto Assets" framework — proposed by SEC Chairman Paul Atkins, building on Commissioner Hester Peirce's earlier Token Safe Harbor. It contemplates a startup exemption, a fundraising exemption, and an investment-contract safe harbor clarifying when tokens are not securities (WP §12.8).

Why DGD aligns (state these as design facts)

The Interpretive Release defines a digital commodity as (WP §12.7):

"a crypto asset intrinsically linked to and deriving its value from the programmatic operation of a functional crypto system, as well as supply and demand dynamics, rather than from expectations of profit."

DGD fits at the most basic level: - Its value comes from the protocol's operation + supply and demand, with supply fixed at 21 million coins. - Its framing is wealth preservation, not profit expectation (WP §12.7). - The founder self-funded development and donated the coin; there was no capital raise, so DGD doesn't even need to rely on the startup/fundraising exemptions — it satisfies the principles outright, as a distributor, not an issuer (WP §12.8).

So "DGD is a safe harbor coin" = "DGD is designed to qualify as a digital commodity, not a security." That's the accurate, powerful claim.

⚠️ The trap (this is the important part)

The digital-commodity classification hinges on value coming from the protocol "rather than from expectations of profit."

That means: the moment an ambassador frames DGD as a way to make money, they are arguing — in public, on the record — that DGD's value comes from profit expectation. That is the exact opposite of what the safe harbor requires. You wouldn't just be breaking the communications discipline; you'd be handing a regulator the argument that DGD is a security after all.

This is why the discipline and the legal posture reinforce each other:

If you say… You break… …and you also…
"DGD is a safe place to park money / safe haven" the discipline imply it's held for value-protection = investment framing
"Safe harbor = safe investment" the discipline conflate a legal term with a financial claim
"It'll go up, get in" the discipline assert value = profit expectation → attacks the digital-commodity status

The one-liner to remember: Safe harbor is about the law, not your wallet.

✅ How to teach "safe harbor" safely

You're explaining why DGD is structured to not be a security — a genuinely interesting, non-promotional topic.

Safe statements: - "DGD is designed to qualify as a digital commodity — not a security — under the 2026 crypto framework." - "Its value is meant to come from how the protocol works and supply and demand — not from expecting a profit. That's literally the legal test." - "The founder funded it himself and ran no token sale, so it's structured as a distribution, not a fundraise." - "Most coins argue about whether they're securities after launch. DGD was designed around the question from day one."

Unsafe (never): - "It's a safe investment / safe haven / safe bet." ❌ - "Safe harbor means you can't lose." ❌ - "Regulators approved it" — ❌ (it's a designed alignment and a reasoned position, not an approval; the White Paper itself calls its non-security stance "a reasoned legal analysis, not a guarantee," and admits a regulator could disagree — WP §12.16).

Supply, Distribution & Circulation

How DGD coins are released and how the design tries to make the coin actually circulate as money.

Present the mechanics as design. The dollar figures describe how the curve works, not what anyone will earn. See do & don't language.

Proof-of-Participation (PoP): continuous distribution

Most coins concentrate supply among early insiders or large "whales." DGD instead releases coins continuously as the network grows, driven by one number: N, the current total number of users (WP Abstract, §5).

How it works each time someone joins: 1. A new user signs up → N increases by one. 2. The protocol advances the price one step along a fixed curve. 3. A small number of coins is released from the treasury, split equally among all current users (WP Abstract).

Because the per-user share is split across everyone, the number of coins released per signup shrinks as the network grows. The very first signup (when N is near 1,000) releases about 88.6 coins; near the target it approaches zero (WP §5). The per-signup releases across the whole journey sum to exactly 11,712,952 coins (WP §5).

Milestone Value (WP)
Starting users 1,000
Target users 80,000,000
Starting price ~$3.40
Ending price $100,000
Max circulating supply at target 19,000,000
Coin release after target Ends permanently

At 80 million users, exactly 19 million coins are in circulation and release ends forever (WP Abstract, §5).

⚠️ The $3.40→$100,000 curve is a distribution mechanism, not a forecast. Saying "get in early before it hits $100k" is a compliance violation. Say instead: "The price moves along a fixed curve as the network grows — it's set by the protocol, not by trading."

How participation works mechanically (for accurate explainers)

  • Participants use a DigitalGoldX web account as the interface to "validate" as the network grows; they pre-load funds and validate up to their per-user share each release (WP §5, §10).
  • Validated coins are delivered to the participant's QT wallet, which only they control — coins arrive even if the web account is unavailable (WP §5).
  • There's a $20 minimum entry threshold (WP §12.14). Partial validation is possible if a balance only covers part of a share.

You don't need to teach all the mechanics in a video — but get them right if you do.

Single-price architecture (the circulation engine)

Here's the design idea that distinguishes DGD from every other coin (WP §8, §9):

  • Every other coin has a fluctuating bid/ask price. A merchant who accepts it faces a choice: hold and eat the volatility, or convert to dollars immediately. The rational merchant converts — so the coin transacts once and exits. It never became money.
  • DGD eliminates bid/ask trading via cooperating-venue exclusivity agreements, producing a single stable price. A merchant can hold DGD and pay suppliers in DGD. The supplier does the same. The chain continues link by link.

When the chain runs all the way to the raw-material owner, "DGD has become money" in the operational sense (WP Abstract, §9).

The lumber example (WP §10): the White Paper walks through dimensional lumber from retail point-of-sale back through wholesaler, manufacturer, and raw-material extraction to show what full supply-chain circulation looks like. This concrete example makes excellent video material.

Teaching angles

  • "Why most crypto payments aren't really money" (the convert-to-dollars problem)
  • "What if everyone who joined a currency got a share — automatically?" (PoP split)
  • "The whale problem, and one attempt to fix it" (continuous vs. front-loaded distribution)

Valuation: The CFV Formula & the DGSB Benchmark

How DGD frames "what a cryptocurrency is actually worth." Strong explainer material because it's analytical, not promotional.

Teach this as a measurement framework, the way you'd explain how the meter or kilogram is defined. Do not use it to argue DGD is "undervalued" or will rise — that crosses into investment framing. See do & don't language.

The core analogy (use this on camera)

Every measurement system needs a fixed standard. The meter is defined by the speed of light; the kilogram by the Planck constant. They don't change just because the thing being measured changes (WP §3.1). Crypto never had such a standard — so Bitcoin's price became the only signal, and every other coin traded as a leveraged bet on Bitcoin (WP §1).

The Foundation proposes one: the Digital Gold Standard Benchmark (DGSB).

The DGSB — a frozen snapshot

In December 2024, Bitcoin first touched $100,000, at a market cap of about $1.983 trillion — valued not by a Reddit thread but by the world's largest asset managers and the regulated ETF infrastructure (WP §3.2). The DGSB freezes that moment as a fixed reference point. It does not track Bitcoin's price afterward; it's a snapshot (WP §3.3).

The benchmark captures four measurable fundamentals:

Metric Bitcoin @ Dec 2024 Weight in CFV
Adoption (unique holders) ~80 million 70%
Annual Transactions ~6.09 billion 10%
Annual Transaction Value ~$13.49 trillion 10%
Active Developers ~905 contributors 10%

(WP §3.2, §4.) Adoption dominates because a currency's worth comes mostly from how many people actually use it.

The CFV formula — plain version

Crypto Fair Value (CFV) measures any coin's fundamentals against the frozen DGSB and produces a "Fair Coin Price." If a coin's fundamentals improve relative to the benchmark, its Fair Coin Price rises; if they deteriorate, it falls. Bitcoin itself is measured against the benchmark on equal terms — no special treatment (WP §3.3).

This mirrors how gold worked historically: gold didn't change to flatter the currencies measured against it; currencies rose or fell on their own merits (WP §3.3).

Why it's good content: it reframes "is this coin overpriced?" from vibes into a transparent, reproducible calculation anyone can verify with public data (WP §1). That's a genuinely interesting, non-salesy story.

Teaching angles

  • "Crypto has no ruler — here's why that's a problem." (the missing-standard idea)
  • "Why every altcoin is basically a bet on Bitcoin." (structural dependency, WP §1)
  • "How do you measure what a coin is really worth? Four numbers." (the CFV metrics)
  • "They froze Bitcoin's best day into a permanent yardstick." (the DGSB snapshot)

Hard line for ambassadors

You may explain the formula and the metrics. You may not: - claim DGD is "undervalued" or "will reach $X," - compare CFV outputs to argue someone should acquire DGD, - imply the framework predicts profit.

Keep it at: "This is the method the framework uses to assign a fair value."

What Actually Makes Short-Form Spread (2026)

The mechanics of reach — applied to educational DGD content. None of this requires hype; the best explainers go viral on curiosity, not promises.

The 1-second truth

The old "first 3 seconds" rule was built for 2018–2022 long-form discovery. On a swipeable short-form feed in 2026, the keep-or-scroll decision happens at or before the 1-second mark. Your first frame + first words are the whole ballgame. Over 70% of viral shorts had a strong hook in the first seconds, and videos with strong hooks see 3–5× higher completion. → hooks library

What the algorithm rewards (Shorts, 2026)

Modern short-form ranking weighs, roughly in order: 1. Replay rate — did they watch it again? 2. Watch-through / completion — did they finish? 3. Click-through / "stop scroll" — did the first frame stop them? 4. Shares — did they send it to someone? 5. Like-to-view ratio and comments.

Notably, for Shorts, raw watch time matters less than replays and shares. That favors short, dense, re-watchable explainers — perfect for one-idea money concepts.

The five levers you control

1. Hook (stop the scroll)

One second to create an information gap or curiosity loop. The three most reliably viral 2026 hook types: Contrarian Claim, Mistake Warning, List Tease. → hooks

2. Retention (keep them)

  • One idea per video. Cut everything else.
  • Visual change every ~2 seconds.
  • "Open loop" early, close it at the end ("…but here's the part nobody mentions →").
  • Captions always (mute viewing).

3. Payoff (reward them)

Deliver the promised insight clearly. A satisfied viewer replays, shares, and follows. Educational content has a built-in advantage: people share things that make them look smart.

4. Shareability (multiply them)

Make it "send this to someone" content: a surprising fact about inflation, a clean mental model, a myth busted. "Did you know the dollar lost 96% of its value since 1913?" is eminently shareable.

5. Consistency (compound them)

Post regularly (series help). The algorithm and your audience both reward a recognizable, repeated format. → content calendar

Educational virality without hype (your lane)

You don't need "🚀 to the moon" energy — that's banned anyway (discipline). Your viral engine is: - Curiosity ("Is this even money?") - Counter-intuition ("Most crypto payments aren't actually money. Here's the test.") - Usefulness ("The 6 things real money needs") - Clarity (make a hard idea feel easy)

Science/finance explainer creators prove this works at scale without selling anything.

Test & iterate

  • Check the retention graph for the exact drop-off second; fix that beat next time.
  • A/B the hook and thumbnail — same video, two openings.
  • Track CTR, replay, share more than raw views.
  • Double down on whichever topic and format overperforms.

Story Structures for 30–60s Explainers

Reusable skeletons. Drop a DGD topic into any of these and you have a script.

1. Hook → Gap → Payoff → Loop (the workhorse)

  • Hook (0–1s): scroll-stopper. → hooks
  • Gap (1–5s): name the thing they don't know ("but here's what nobody explains…").
  • Payoff (5–35s): deliver the insight, one idea, with visuals.
  • Loop (35–45s): reopen curiosity + soft CTA ("follow for the rest / read the white paper").

Example (Scarcity): "Saving money is making you poorer 〔hook〕. We're taught saving is safe — but the supply keeps growing 〔gap〕. The US money supply grew ~7,000% in a century; more units chasing the same goods means each unit buys less. Code can cap supply instead — like a 21-million limit that can't be raised 〔payoff〕. That's one of six things 'real money' needs — follow for the other five 〔loop〕."

2. Myth → Reality → Why it matters

  • State a common belief → flip it → explain the consequence.
  • Example: "More coins means more value 〔myth〕 — actually a fixed cap matters more than the number 〔reality〕 — because predictability is what makes money hold value 〔why〕."

3. Question → Exploration → Answer

  • Open with a real question, explore 2–3 beats, land the answer.
  • Example: "What actually makes something money? Menger said: the most saleable good wins. Durable, divisible, portable, scarce, wanted. Gold hit all five. Now code can too."

4. Number → Breakdown → Takeaway

  • Lead with a striking stat, decompose it, leave one lesson.
  • Example: "4 numbers decide a coin's fair value: adoption — weighted 70% — plus transactions, transaction value, and active developers. Mostly, it's about how many people really use it."

5. Then vs. Now (history hook)

  • Past → present parallel. Great for monetary history.
  • Example: "1920s stock market: tons of speculation, no way to value anything. 2026 crypto: same problem. Here's one attempt at a yardstick."

6. The Analogy Build

  • One sticky analogy, extended.
  • Example: "A ruler that changes length every day would be useless. That's what inflation does to the dollar as a measure of value."

7. Listicle (series engine)

  • "N things…" with quick beats; each item can also be its own video.
  • Example: "6 rules of real money: scarcity, stable pricing, free adoption, decentralized governance, freedom to transact, circulation."

Structural rules that apply to all

  • One idea per video. If you have two, make two videos.
  • Front-load value. Don't save the good part for the end on short-form.
  • Show, don't just say. Pair each line with a visual change. → editing
  • End educational. CTA is "learn more / follow / read the white paper," never "buy." → discipline

The Golden Rules of DGD Communication

The single most important page in this wiki. These rules come directly from the Foundation's own commitments in the White Paper. They are not optional.

Where these rules come from

White Paper §12.11, "Marketing and Communications Discipline," states the Foundation's communications:

"…do not characterize DGD as an investment, do not project specific price appreciation, do not promise returns from any Foundation activity, and do not solicit participation on the basis of expected profits."

It continues: communications describe DGD's design, the CFV framework, the monetary-reform mission, and the mechanics of validation — so that participants decide "based on their own evaluation of the design and the framework, not on the basis of Foundation representations about profit potential" (WP §12.11).

This discipline explicitly applies to the Foundation's authorized communications channels — and as a rewarded ambassador you are speaking into that same public record.

The Five Golden Rules

1. Never call it an investment

DGD is money / a monetary system / a currency design — not an investment, asset to flip, or opportunity. The White Paper says plainly: "This is not a speculative instrument" (WP Abstract).

2. Never predict the price

No "it'll hit $X," no "going to $100k," no "early," no charts implying future gains. The price curve is a distribution mechanism, not a forecast (WP §5). You may explain how the curve works; you may not say where it's going for your benefit.

3. Never promise returns or profit

No "returns," "ROI," "passive income," "you'll make money," "I made money." No party earns income from DGD's operation by design — there are no staking rewards and fees are burned (WP Abstract, §12).

4. Never solicit on the basis of profit

Don't say "buy now," "get in before…," "don't miss out." You can invite people to learn, read the white paper, and evaluate the design — not to chase gains.

5. Always stay educational and honest

Describe design, economics, history, and mechanics. Acknowledge uncertainty openly — the White Paper itself does: it calls its non-security position "a reasoned legal analysis, not a guarantee" and admits a regulator could reach a different conclusion (WP §12.16). Honesty is on-brand.

What you CAN do (lots)

  • Explain inflation, sound money, and Austrian economics.
  • Teach the Six Pillars, the CFV/DGSB framework, PoP distribution, single-price circulation.
  • Give accurate, sourced "how it's designed to work" breakdowns.
  • Share your honest interest in the ideas.
  • Point people to the white paper and official channels to judge for themselves.

The mindset

You're a teacher and explainer, like a good science communicator — not a salesperson and not a hype account. The best DGD content would be valuable to someone who never acquires a single coin. If your video only makes sense as a reason to buy, rewrite it.

If someone asks you "will I make money?"

Safe answer: "I can't tell you that, and I won't — I'm not a financial advisor and this isn't investment advice. What I can do is explain how the system is designed. Whether it's right for you is your call after your own research."

FTC Disclosure — When and How to Say "Sponsored"

If you receive anything of value for posting — DGD recognition, free products, perks — you have a material connection and US FTC rules require you to disclose it clearly. This is separate from, and in addition to, the Foundation's communications discipline.

When disclosure is required

You must disclose if you get any of: DGD recognition through the promotional pathway, payment, free products, event invites, discounts, or "any other form of value exchange." The FTC treats a missing disclosure on a financial/crypto promotion as high-risk because of potential financial harm.

When in doubt: disclose. There's no penalty for over-disclosing.

How to disclose correctly (2026 standard)

The FTC's bar in 2026 is that disclosures must be obvious to an average viewer immediately — unavoidable, before they engage, not buried.

Do all three for short-form video:

  1. Spoken — say it out loud in the first few seconds: "This is a sponsored educational video — I take part in Digital Gold's ambassador program."
  2. On-screen text — a clear overlay (high-contrast, readable) in the opening, e.g. "Sponsored / Ambassador content."
  3. Caption — put #Ad or #Sponsored at the very start of the caption, not after a wall of hashtags.

Use the platform's built-in tool too: - Instagram/Facebook: turn on the Paid Partnership label. → platform-policies.md#instagram-reels - TikTok: toggle the branded content / paid partnership disclosure. - YouTube: tick "contains paid promotion." - X: use the paid partnership label.

What does NOT count (FTC will reject these)

  • ❌ "#ad" at the end of 15 hashtags.
  • ❌ Tiny, low-contrast text that flashes by.
  • ❌ "Thanks to my partners" with no clear sponsorship language.
  • ❌ Disclosure only in a pinned comment.

Why this is doubly important for DGD

A crypto/financial promotion with a missing disclosure is exactly what the FTC has been enforcing against (celebrity crypto settlements in 2024–2025). Penalties can run $51,000+ per violation and stack across every post. And remember: a disclosure says you were rewarded — it does not let you make investment claims. You still cannot promise returns or predict price. Disclosure + discipline, always both.

Plain-English disclosure scripts (copy these)

  • Opening line: "Quick heads-up: I'm part of the Digital Gold ambassador program, so this is sponsored — and it's educational, not financial advice."
  • On-screen (0–3s): "SPONSORED · AMBASSADOR · NOT FINANCIAL ADVICE"
  • Caption start: "#Ad — educational, not financial advice. ..."

AI-Content Disclosure — Labeling Synthetic Media

Every major platform now requires you to label realistic AI-generated or altered content. Since this wiki is all about making video with AI tools, this matters on most of your posts.

The simple rule

Label it when AI makes something realistic that a viewer could mistake for real — a real-looking person, voice, place, or event. You generally don't need to label AI used as production assistance (idea generation, scripting, outlines, captions, basic editing).

AI use Disclose?
AI script / ideas / outline / title ❌ No
AI captions / basic edit / b-roll cleanup ❌ No (production assistance)
AI voiceover that sounds like a real person ✅ Yes
AI avatar / talking head / face ✅ Yes
Realistic AI video of people/places/events ✅ Yes
Altering real footage to look different than reality ✅ Yes
Obvious cartoons, clearly-stylized animation, abstract motion graphics Usually ❌ (not "realistic")

Disclosure of "meaningfully altered or synthetically generated" realistic content is mandatory (fully enforced since May 21, 2025; policy refreshed Jan 2026). At upload, in YouTube Studio → details → "Altered content" → Yes. YouTube adds a "Modified or Synthetic" note. If you use YouTube's own built-in generative effects, disclosure is handled automatically. As of May 2026, YouTube runs automatic AI detection and can apply the label even if you don't self-disclose. Repeatedly skipping required disclosure can mean forced labels, warnings, removal, or YPP suspension. Good news: labeling does not reduce reach or monetization. Takes <30 seconds.

TikTok

Toggle the AI-generated content label when content is realistic AI. TikTok now auto-detects synthetic media via C2PA Content Credentials and will auto-apply a "Contains AI-generated content" label even without your input — so self-label first. For a paid partnership, remember you owe two disclosures: the Branded Content toggle and the AI label. AI-assisted text (scripts, hashtags) is exempt.

Instagram / Meta

Meta applies "AI info" labeling to realistic AI imagery/video and expects creators to disclose. As of 2026, AI-content disclosure is mandatory for all advertising/boosted content across Facebook and Instagram, applied globally. Add your own on-screen note for realistic synthetic media.

The EU AI Act's transparency rules require AI-generated content to be labeled in a machine-readable, detectable way and become fully applicable on 2 August 2026. Any platform operating in the EU must have compliant labeling in place — another reason to disclose by default rather than rely on a single platform toggle.

Belt-and-suspenders best practice

Why bother

  • It's required; non-compliance risks removal and demonetization.

  • It pairs naturally with your FTC disclosure and "not financial advice" note.

Three rulebooks govern every video you make. Break any one and your content gets removed, demonetized, or worse — and it can create legal exposure for you and the Foundation.

  1. Fact-check against the wiki. Cross-check claims against dgd/. The AI may invent numbers — your White Paper figures are the source of truth.
  2. Run the do-and-don't pass. Scan for banned words. → do & don't language
  3. Add disclosures if you have a material connection. → FTC
  4. Tighten the hook. The first line earns the next. → hooks

Script length cheat-sheet

  • 15–30s: one idea, one hook, one payoff. Best for TikTok/Reels.
  • 30–60s: a small "explainer arc." Great for YouTube Shorts and X.
  • ~120 words ≈ ~45–50 seconds of natural narration.

Thumbnails & title cards (the compliance trap)

Your thumbnail and on-screen title are subject to the same rules as your script. A perfect video with a "🚀 DGD TO $100K 🤑" thumbnail still violates the communications discipline and platform financial rules.

Safe thumbnail/title formulas: - "Why money loses value 📉" (curiosity, no claim) - "The 6 rules of REAL money" - "Is this even money? 🤔" - "Inflation, explained in 30s"

Unsafe: anything with price targets, rockets-to-the-moon, dollar-stacks-with-gains framing, "get rich," "early."

Quick design tips

  • Big, legible text (3–5 words max), high contrast.
  • One focal subject; faces/eyes draw clicks but keep it honest.
  • Consistent brand look across a series builds recognition.
  • Free design app: Canva (templates, text, simple editing).

Images you'll reuse a lot

The golden licensing rules

  1. Download from the official source and save the license note for each track.
  2. "Free on YouTube" ≠ "free everywhere." Confirm the license covers all platforms and short-form before you cross-post to TikTok/Reels/X. Pixabay and Uppbeat are safer for multi-platform.
  3. Trending audio is a trap. Using a platform's trending commercial song can boost reach but is usually licensed only for personal, non-commercial use. Since ambassador content is commercial/branded, avoid trending commercial tracks — use cleared libraries instead.
  4. Attribution where required goes in the description/caption.

Sound design that lifts retention (free)

  • A subtle "whoosh" on transitions, a soft "ding" on key points (CapCut sound effects, Pixabay SFX).
  • Keep music under the voice: duck music to ~15–20% when narration plays.
  • Match energy to topic: calm/curious for explainers, not hype EDM (which also clashes with the educational tone).

Quick mix checklist

  • Voice clear and on top; music ducked under it.
  • No sudden volume jumps between clips.
  • Normalize final mix (~-14 LUFS).
  • Test on phone speakers, not just headphones.

End-to-End Free Workflows

Three complete, $0 pipelines from idea to posted video. Pick the one that fits your time and comfort.

Workflow A — "Faceless Explainer" (fastest, no camera)

Time: ~1–2 hours · Best for: TikTok, Reels, Shorts, X

  1. Script — ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini with a compliance-locked prompt from the prompt library. Fact-check vs dgd/.
  2. Voice — ElevenLabs free or Edge Read Aloud. Label as AI if synthetic.
  3. Visuals — abstract b-roll from Veo/Kling + title cards from Bing/Firefly. Gold, inflation, supply-chain themes.
  4. Edit + captions — CapCut: assemble, auto-caption, proofread, add disclosure overlays.
  5. Music — YouTube Audio Library / Pixabay, ducked under voice.
  6. Compliance pass — pre-publish checklist.
  7. Post — vertical 1080×1920; front-load disclosure; native captions/hashtags per platform specs.

Workflow B — "Talking Head + B-roll" (most trust)

Time: ~1–2 hours · Best for: building a personal ambassador brand

  1. Script / bullet points — AI outline, but speak in your own words.
  2. Film yourself on a phone (good light, quiet room). Your real voice = no AI-voice disclosure needed and higher trust.
  3. B-roll overlays — drop AI/stock clips over your talking head to illustrate (gold, charts, supply chain).
  4. Edit + captions — CapCut: punch-ins, captions, disclosure overlays.
  5. Music + SFX — subtle, cleared library.
  6. Compliance pass + post.

For financial-adjacent education, a real human face often outperforms faceless AI for trust and follows. Consider B for "who am I / why I care" content and A for pure explainers.

Workflow C — "Batch & Schedule" (consistency at scale)

Time: a half-day to make a week of content

  1. Plan a week from the content calendar (e.g., a Six Pillars series).
  2. Batch-script all 5–7 videos in one AI session (compliance-locked prompt).
  3. Batch-produce: generate all voiceovers, then all b-roll, then edit back-to-back in CapCut (reuse a template project).
  4. Compliance pass on each before scheduling.
  5. Schedule: use native schedulers (YouTube, Meta Business Suite) or a free tier (Postiz / Buffer). Mind X automation rules — no spammy duplicate posting; tailor captions per platform.

Reusable project hygiene

  • Keep a CapCut template with your caption style + disclosure overlays pre-built — drop in new audio/clips each time.
  • Maintain a /raw asset folder (clips, voiceovers, title cards) to remix later.
  • Save a "compliance overlay" PNG ("SPONSORED · NOT FINANCIAL ADVICE") to reuse.

The non-negotiable last step

Every workflow ends at the pre-publish checklist. No exceptions.
